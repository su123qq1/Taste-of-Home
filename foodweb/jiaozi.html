Jiaozi

traditional Chinese dumplings

What is Jiaozi?

Jiaozi is the formal name of Chinese dumplings. They are popular major dishes in China and in some East Asian countries, but each country name them differently. For example, Korean dumplings are called Mandu, Janpese dumplings are called Gyoze, and Vietnamese dumplings are called Banh Bot Loc.

There are two special times in which Chinese people eat Jiaozi. One is Winter Solstice, also called Dongzhi Festival; another one is Lunar New Year's Eve. Regarding its symbolic meaning in China, Jiaozi represents "happy family reunion" and "good luck" in the upcoming year. It also means the change between the old and the new. Usually, it is served with a vinegar dipping sauce.

Where Jiaozi originated?

As tracing back the , a famous physician of traditional Chinese medicine, named Zhang Zhongjing, created Jiaozi for medicine purposes during Eastern Han Dynasty (25 - 220 AD). During the Three Kingdoms period (220 - 280 AD), it had become a kind of home-made food.

How to make Jiaozi?

The wrapper of Jiaozi is made by flour from bun into thinly rolled pieces of small rounded doughs. The fillings typically consist of a ground meat and/or vegetable. After putting the fillings in the middle, the wrapper then is sealed by using fingers to fold and press the edges together.

The common cooking methods include to be boiled, pan-fired, and steamed. Also, although each family has their preferred ingredients collocation, there are not that many differences among preparations. Following are four popular kinds of home-made fillings.

  1. Chinese cabbage and pork
  2. Chinese celery and pork
  3. Corn and shrimp
  4. Onion and beef
  5. Green Chinese onion and lamb
